# PIAGETS: Continual Learning for Dynamical Systems Reconstruction

**Parameter-Importance-weighted Attractor-Graph Embedding for Task Schematization**

*Working note on the CL approach, theoretical correspondences, and the signature-Fisher framework.*

---

## 1. Setting and Motivation

### 1.1 The continual DSR problem

A stream of datasets $\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2, \ldots$ arrives sequentially, each generated by a distinct dynamical system $F_t$. At each step we fit an AL-RNN to $\mathcal{D}_t$ and must decide:

$$k^* = \arg\min_k\, d\!\left(\Phi(F_t),\, c_k\right), \qquad
\begin{cases} d < \tau: & \textbf{assimilate} \text{ (reuse schema } k^*) \\ d \geq \tau: & \textbf{accommodate} \text{ (spawn new schema)} \end{cases}$$

The challenge is **catastrophic forgetting**: gradient updates for task $t$ destroy the representation of earlier tasks in shared parameters.

Why a single model (shared parameters) rather than separate models per task?

1. **Recognition requires a common embedding space.** Separate models live in incomparable latent gauges; you cannot compute $d(\Phi_1, \Phi_2)$ between two models' weight matrices directly. Shared structure puts every task into the same signature space.
2. **Assimilation is only cheap with sharing.** If task $t$ belongs to an existing schema, reusing that schema's subspace costs only fitting a small affine chart ($\ll$ full retraining). Without sharing there is nothing to reuse.
3. **The schema library is the scientific output.** "Patient A's brain cycles between schemas 2 and 4" is a meaningful statement; "Patient A has models #47 and #83" is not.

### 1.2 Why standard EWC fails for DSR

Elastic Weight Consolidation (Kirkpatrick et al. 2017) penalises parameter changes weighted by the Fisher information:
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{EWC}} = \mathcal{L}_t + \frac{\lambda}{2}\sum_i F_i(\theta_i - \theta_i^*)^2, \qquad F_i = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\!\left(\frac{\partial \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right)^{\!2}\right]$$

$F_i$ measures sensitivity of the **one-step prediction loss** to $\theta_i$. For DSR this is the wrong target:

- Parameters that define **orbit structure** (spectral radius of $A$, the nonlinear coupling columns of $W_{:,:P}$) may have low $F_i$ because stochastic observations make the one-step gradient noisy and the Fisher poorly estimated over long chaotic trajectories.
- Parameters that only affect short-horizon predictions (fast weights) get high $F_i$ even if they are irrelevant to the attractor type.
- The Fisher matrix for the full $\theta$ is $M^2 \times M^2$ — dense and expensive to estimate from noisy data.

The professor's observation — *subspace allocation works, EWC does not* — is a direct consequence: subspace methods make forgetting **structurally impossible** (you cannot modify a frozen subspace), so no Fisher estimate is needed.

## 2. SLAO: the Base CL Method

**"Merge before Forget: A Single LoRA Continual Learning via Continual Merging"** (Qiao & Mahdavi, arXiv 2512.23017).

SLAO maintains a single LoRA $\Delta W = B_{\text{merge}} A_{\text{merge}}$ that accumulates all tasks. At each task boundary it applies two asymmetric rules:

### 2.1 Orthogonal initialisation (QR)

Before training on task $t$, initialise the new task's $A$ component orthogonally to the previous task's fine-tuned $A$:
$$Q_t R_t = \mathrm{QR}\!\left((A_{\mathrm{ft},t-1})^\top\right), \qquad A^{(0)}_{\mathrm{ft},t} = Q_t^\top$$

This ensures $A^{(0)}_{\mathrm{ft},t}(A^{(0)}_{\mathrm{ft},t})^\top = I_r$ and, by construction, the new task starts in a subspace orthogonal to the previous task's "basis directions." The $A$ matrix thus builds a growing orthogonal basis over time.

### 2.2 Asymmetric merge

At the end of task $t$:

$$A_{\mathrm{merge},t} = A_{\mathrm{ft},t} \qquad \text{(direct replacement)}$$

$$B_{\mathrm{merge},t} = B_{\mathrm{merge},t-1} + \lambda^{(t)}\!\left(B_{\mathrm{ft},t} - B_{\mathrm{merge},t-1}\right), \qquad \lambda^{(t)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}}$$

The $A$ basis is replaced (it gets a fresh orthogonal direction each task). The $B$ coefficients are accumulated via a time-decaying EMA: early tasks get small updates (downweighted by $1/\sqrt{t}$), preserving earlier knowledge.

### 2.3 EWC extension (your contribution)

Replace the scalar $\lambda^{(t)}$ with a **per-element** learning rate that decays faster for coefficients that mattered for past tasks:

$$B_{\mathrm{merge},t} = B_{\mathrm{merge},t-1} + \alpha \odot (B_{\mathrm{ft},t} - B_{\mathrm{merge},t-1})$$

$$\alpha_{jk} = \frac{\lambda(t)}{1 + \beta\, \widetilde{F}_{jk}}, \qquad \lambda(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}}$$

where $\widetilde{F}_{jk}$ is the Fisher importance of element $(j,k)$ in $B$. Elements of $B$ with high importance (large $\widetilde{F}_{jk}$) receive smaller updates — the denominator $(1 + \beta F_{jk})$ acts as an elastic spring. This is structurally EWC applied to $B$ alone rather than the full weight matrix, which has two advantages: $B$ is $M \times r$ (much smaller than $M \times M$), and the Fisher estimates are more reliable over fewer parameters.

The remaining question — what $\widetilde{F}_{jk}$ should measure — is addressed in §4.

## 3. Formal Correspondence: Rank-$r$ LoRA $\longleftrightarrow$ $P$-Unit AL-RNN

### 3.1 The AL-RNN as dynamic LoRA

Recall the AL-RNN:
$$z_{t+1} = \underbrace{\mathrm{diag}(a)}_{\text{diagonal recurrence}} z_t + W g(z_t) + h, \qquad g(z)_i = \begin{cases} \mathrm{relu}(z_i) & i < P \\ z_i & i \geq P \end{cases}$$

Define the **always-linear weight** $\bar{W} = \mathrm{diag}(a) + W$ (what the dynamics would be if all units were linear). Then:

$$z_{t+1} = \bar{W} z_t + W\!\left(g(z_t) - z_t\right) + h = \bar{W} z_t + \underbrace{W_{:,:P}}_{=:\, B \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times P}} \underbrace{\left(\mathrm{relu}(z_{:P,t}) - z_{:P,t}\right)}_{=:\, \phi_t \in \mathbb{R}^P} + h$$

Since $\mathrm{relu}(z) - z = -\min(0,z) = \mathrm{relu}(-z)$:

$$\boxed{z_{t+1} = \bar{W}\, z_t + B\, \phi(z_t) + h, \qquad \phi(z) = \mathrm{relu}(-z_{:P}) \geq 0}$$

This is a **state-conditional rank-$P$ LoRA**:
- **Base operator**: $\bar{W}$ (the task-invariant linear dynamics)
- **Low-rank update**: $B \cdot \phi(z_t)$, rank $\leq P$

Compare to standard LoRA with a fixed linear projection $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times M}$:

| | Static LoRA (rank $r$) | AL-RNN ($P$ nonlinear units) |
|---|---|---|
| Low-rank output matrix | $B \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r}$ | $W_{:,:P} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times P}$ |
| Low-rank input | $Az_t$ — fixed linear projection | $\phi(z_t)$ — nonlinear, state-dependent |
| Effect on dynamics | Single fixed rank-$r$ correction | Region-switching: $2^P$ possible corrections |
| Structural rank | $r$ (constant) | $\leq P$ (varies along trajectory) |
| Full-rank limit | $r = M$ → full fine-tune | $P = M$ → full PLRNN |

### 3.2 The mixture-of-LoRAs interpretation

In region $\Omega$ (fixed activation pattern $D_\Omega = \mathrm{diag}(\mathbb{1}[z_{i,t}>0])_{i<P}$):
$$\phi(z_t) = -D_\Omega^{\mathrm{neg}} z_{:P,t}, \qquad D_\Omega^{\mathrm{neg}} = \mathrm{diag}(\mathbb{1}[z_{i,t}<0])_{i<P}$$

So within region $\Omega$, the AL-RNN IS a LoRA with $A = -D_\Omega^{\mathrm{neg}} E_{:P}$ (where $E_{:P} \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times M}$ extracts the first $P$ components):
$$W_\Omega = \bar{W} - B D_\Omega^{\mathrm{neg}} E_{:P}$$

This is exactly the region Jacobian $\mathrm{diag}(a) + W D_\Omega$. The AL-RNN implements a **mixture of $2^P$ LoRA adapters** where the trajectory selects which adapter is active at each step. At most $P$ of the $2^P$ adapters contribute any particular step (at most $P$ units can be in the negative half-space simultaneously if they're all independent).

### 3.3 Rank and $P$: a precise analogy

**Static LoRA**: The rank $r$ bounds the dimension of the adaptation subspace. All tasks share the same rank-$r$ correction applied uniformly to all inputs.

**AL-RNN $P$**: The $P$ switching boundaries bound the dimension of the nonlinear correction at any instant, but the effective correction *changes* with the trajectory, carving out a different affine subspace in each region. This is strictly more expressive: $P$ parameters encode up to $2^P$ distinct linear behaviors, whereas LoRA rank $r$ encodes exactly one.

**The key duality**: LoRA's rank $r$ ↔ AL-RNN's $P$ *as a capacity parameter*, but with the AL-RNN trading the fixed linear $A$ for a nonlinear, state-switching selection mechanism.

### 3.4 Adaptive $P$ (analogous to AdaLoRA)

AdaLoRA (Zhang et al. 2023) adaptively allocates rank budget across layers using SVD: $\Delta W = P\Lambda Q$, prune dimensions with $\lambda_i < \epsilon$.

For the AL-RNN, the natural adaptive-$P$ criterion operates on $B = W_{:,:P}$:

**SVD-based (post-hoc):** Compute $W_{:,:P} = U\Sigma V^\top$. Units $i$ with $\sigma_i < \epsilon$ contribute negligible nonlinearity; set them to linear ($P_{\mathrm{eff}} = \#\{\sigma_i \geq \epsilon\}$).

**Closed-form (data-driven, before training):** Observe the data trajectory, PCA-reduce, count distinct sign-change patterns across components. The number of distinct patterns $N_\Omega$ gives $P = \lceil \log_2 N_\Omega \rceil$ — the minimum number of ReLU units needed to represent the observed partition.

**Regularization-based (during training):** The `region_reg` entropy term $-H(p_i)$ forces each unit toward $p_i = 0.5$ (maximum switching). After training, units with $p_i \approx 0$ or $p_i \approx 1$ are degenerate (never flip) and can be removed: $P_{\mathrm{eff}}$ = number of units with $|p_i - 0.5| < \delta$.

**Full-rank LoRA (HiRA) $\equiv P = M$ (full PLRNN).** Experiments confirm: raising $P$ to $M=16$ on EEG didn't help — the bottleneck was reconstruction fidelity, not capacity. HiRA thus recovers the PLRNN limit, which we've tested.

### 3.5 SLAO for AL-RNN

SLAO maps cleanly onto the $B = W_{:,:P}$ structure. Apply QR to the nonlinear columns:
$$W_{:,:P} = QR, \qquad Q \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times P} \text{ orthonormal},\quad R \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times P}$$

At task boundary:
- **Freeze** $Q$ (the schema's switching subspace, $MP$ parameters)
- **Update only** $R$ for new tasks ($P^2 \ll MP$ parameters for typical $P \ll M$)
- **EWC on $R$** (not on the full $W$): $F_{R_{ij}}^\Phi = \|\partial\Phi/\partial R_{ij}\|^2$

Since $\partial W_{:,k}/\partial R_{ij} = Q_{:,i}\,\delta_{kj}$ (clean chain rule through the fixed $Q$), the Fisher for $R$ decomposes as projections of the signature gradient onto the frozen basis vectors.

The new task then initializes $Q_{\mathrm{new}}$ via QR of the previous $R$ (following SLAO's orthogonalization principle), grows the basis if needed, and updates $R_{\mathrm{new}}$ with the Fisher-weighted EMA.

## 4. Signature-Fisher: The Proper Formulation

### 4.1 The core idea

The right Fisher importance for DSR-EWC is sensitivity of the **schema embedding** $\Phi(\theta)$ to each parameter, not sensitivity of the one-step prediction loss:

$$F_i^\Phi = \left\|\frac{\partial \Phi(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2$$

With block weights matching the embedding (Layer 1):

$$F_i^\Phi = \sum_{b} w_b \left\|\frac{\partial \Phi_b(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2, \qquad w_b = \begin{cases} 1 & b \in \text{topological} \\ \gamma & b \in \text{rate} \\ g & b \in \text{geometry} \end{cases}$$

Three things this fixes simultaneously vs standard EWC:

1. **Stochasticity**: $\Phi$ integrates over long trajectories (Lyapunov from 2500+ steps), averaging out observation noise. $F_i^\Phi$ is stable where $F_i^{\text{pred}}$ is not.
2. **Mismatch**: Parameters that define orbit structure (spectral radius of $A$, nonlinear coupling $W_{:,:P}$) get high $F_i^\Phi$; parameters that only affect one-step fitting get appropriate (possibly low) weight.
3. **Gauge-awareness**: If $\Phi$ is gauge-free (invariant under latent basis rotations), then parameter changes that amount to basis rotations have $\partial\Phi/\partial\theta_i \approx 0$ and automatically get near-zero importance — no explicit gauge handling needed.

### 4.2 Differentiable blocks (exact gradients)

These can be directly autodifferentiated through PyTorch:

**Linear core spectrum** (`eq_spectrum`): eigenvalues of $A_{P:,P:} + W_{P:,P:}$. Standard eigenvalue differentiation. $O(M^3)$.

**Lyapunov spectrum** (`lyap_spectrum`, `lyap_ky`, `lyap_signs`): QR iteration on exact piecewise-linear Jacobians $J_t = \mathrm{diag}(a) + W \cdot \mathrm{diag}(g'(z_t))$. The iteration is purely matrix multiplications and QR decompositions — fully autodiff. Cost $O(T \cdot M^3)$ for $T$ steps; practically $T \approx 1000$–$2500$.

**Geometry blocks** (`geom_spectral_perchannel`, `geom_spectral_bands`, etc.): computed from free-run outputs $x_t = z_{t,:d}$ via FFT-based band power. `torch.fft.rfft` is differentiable; band power = sum of squared magnitudes in frequency bins. Backpropagates through the generative pass (standard BPTT over the free-run). Cost $O(T \cdot M^2)$ for the rollout plus $O(T \log T)$ for FFT.

**Region spectral blocks** (`spectral_region`): visit-weighted average of region Jacobian eigenvalues. With soft region membership (see §4.3), fully differentiable.

### 4.3 Differentiable surrogates for discrete/topological blocks

The topological blocks are discrete functions of the activation pattern sequence — not directly differentiable. Three strategies:

**Soft region membership.** Replace the hard $\mathrm{relu}(z_i) \to z_i$ switch with a smooth gate:
$$D_{\Omega,i}^{\mathrm{soft}}(z) = \sigma(\beta z_i), \qquad \beta \gg 1$$

The soft transition matrix becomes:
$$\widetilde{T}_{ij} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \sigma(\beta z_{i,t})\,\sigma(\beta z_{j,t+1})$$

and the **soft topological entropy**:
$$\tilde{h} = \log \rho(\widetilde{T})$$

is differentiable through the spectral radius $\rho$ (Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue, differentiable via implicit differentiation of the characteristic polynomial). Near a bifurcation where a new orbit type emerges, $\rho(\widetilde{T})$ changes rapidly — giving high gradient exactly at schema boundaries, where you want strong regularization.

**Persistent homology gradients.** For the `topo_persistence` block: the free-run trajectory forms a point cloud in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Birth/death times of topological features in the Vietoris-Rips filtration are differentiable w.r.t. pairwise distances almost everywhere (Hofer et al. 2019; now available in `giotto-tda` and `gudhi`). The differentiable surrogate is the $p$-Wasserstein distance between the current persistence diagram and a target diagram — backpropagating through this gives parameter gradients that push the topology toward (or away from) a reference.

**Functional regularization (no gradients needed).** For truly discrete invariants (exact Betti numbers $\beta_k$, exact number of fixed points, exact symbolic graph isomorphism class), evaluate $\Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}(\theta^*)$ once at the end of task $t$ and directly penalise deviation during task $t+1$:
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{topo}} = \left\|\Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\mathrm{soft}}(\theta) - \Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}(\theta^*)\right\|^2$$

using soft surrogates on the left (differentiable, computed during training) anchored to the hard values on the right (evaluated once, not differentiated).

### 4.4 Do we want topology in the Fisher at all?

The EEG results (§6 of `signature_findings.md`) showed topological and rate blocks at chance while geometry dominated. This does NOT mean topology is useless for the Fisher — the contexts are different:

- **EEG sleep staging**: the discriminative information was in the *observation frame* (per-channel spectral), not in attractor topology. Reconstruction collapsed (n_regions=1 in old runs; better now but still noisy), so model-side blocks carry noise.
- **DSR on clean attractors (the CL setting)**: topological blocks ARE discriminative (synthetic experiments: different Betti numbers, different symbolic graphs for different attractor types). This is exactly where CL happens.
- **The Fisher's role**: preserve the current task's schema while learning the next. A limit-cycle schema should have its $\beta_1=1$, one-zero-Lyapunov, periodic symbolic dynamics preserved. These are topological + rate — not geometry.

The EWC penalty on topological blocks has a natural property: in the interior of parameter space (far from bifurcations), topological invariants are **locally constant** in $\theta$, so $\partial\Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}/\partial\theta_i \approx 0$ and the penalty contributes nothing. Near a bifurcation (where the schema type would change), they change rapidly — high gradient exactly where you most need regularization. This is the right behaviour.

**Practical recommendation**: include rate (Lyapunov, linear core) and soft-topological (topological entropy $\tilde{h}$) in the Fisher via autodiff. Include hard topological blocks via functional regularization. Include geometry when the observation frame is semantically meaningful (real sensor data). Exclude geometry for synthetic DSR (gauge-free setting).

### 4.5 Complete signature-Fisher formula

$$F_i^\Phi = \underbrace{\left\|\frac{\partial \lambda_\mathrm{lyap}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2}_{\text{Lyapunov}} + \gamma \underbrace{\left\|\frac{\partial \tilde{h}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2}_{\text{soft topo-entropy}} + \gamma \underbrace{\left\|\frac{\partial \mathrm{spec}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2}_{\text{linear core spectrum}} + g \underbrace{\left\|\frac{\partial \mathrm{geom}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right\|^2}_{\text{geometry (if applicable)}}$$

In the SLAO+EWC adaptation, the Fisher for $R$ (the P×P coefficient matrix in $W_{:,:P} = QR$) drives the per-element merge rate:
$$\alpha_{jk} = \frac{\lambda(t)}{1 + \beta F_{R_{jk}}^\Phi}$$

## 5. The Geometry Question

The finding that per-channel geometry dominated (ARI 0.61 vs 0.00 for model-side) raises a design question: when should geometry enter the embedding and the Fisher?

**When the observation frame is arbitrary** (synthetic DSR, latent variables, different recording setups): geometry is gauge-bearing and should be excluded. Two models of the same attractor recorded in different channel arrangements should be the same schema.

**When the observation frame is semantically meaningful** (EEG with labelled electrodes, fMRI ROIs, structured sensor arrays): channel identity carries functional meaning. A topologically conjugate but spatially misarranged attractor is NOT the same schema — it corresponds to a different configuration of neural circuits. The right equivalence relation is not topological conjugacy but *embedded conjugacy* (conjugacy that additionally preserves the observation map up to a channel-identity-preserving transformation). In this regime:
- Include `geom_spectral_perchannel` in $\Phi$ (it's the most discriminative block)
- Include geometry in the Fisher ($g > 0$) to penalise parameter changes that would shift the observation-space spectral profile
- The `geom_per_channel=True` flag encodes this modelling decision

Note: the geometry blocks are fully differentiable (FFT-based band power through PyTorch's `torch.fft`) and can be computed from the model's free-run, so they slot cleanly into the autodiff Fisher.

## 6. PIAGETS Acronym

The chosen full name is:

> **P**arameter-**I**mportance-weighted **A**ttractor-**G**raph **E**mbedding for **T**ask **S**chematization

Each word earns its place:
- **Parameter-Importance-weighted** — the Fisher weighting of §4, which is the core technical contribution
- **Attractor-Graph** — what is embedded: the symbolic transition graph over visited regions (§5.3 of `signature_overview.md`), plus the broader attractor geometry
- **Embedding** — the signature Φ(θ) maps a trained model to a metric space
- **Task Schematization** — the goal: assigning tasks to reusable schema classes (the assimilate/accommodate decision of §1.1)

The name also references Jean Piaget (1896–1980), whose theory of cognitive development centres on **schemas** (organised patterns of thought), **assimilation** (mapping new experience to an existing schema), and **accommodation** (modifying schemas when existing ones fail).

### 4.5 Complete objective

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PIAGETS}} = \mathcal{L}_t(\theta) + \frac{\lambda}{2}\sum_i F_i^\Phi\, (\theta_i - \theta_i^*)^2 + \mu \left\|\Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\mathrm{soft}}(\theta) - \Phi_{\mathrm{topo}}(\theta^*)\right\|^2$$

## 7. Open Questions and Next Steps

1. **Does signature-Fisher on $R$ give better-conditioned Fisher estimates than EWC on $W$?** The bet: $R$ is $P \times P$ (e.g., $6 \times 6 = 36$ parameters), making the Fisher tractable; the Lyapunov gradient through QR iteration over $R$ is well-conditioned because $Q$ is frozen.

2. **Does the soft topological entropy $\tilde{h} = \log\rho(\widetilde{T})$ give stable gradients?** The spectral radius is non-smooth at eigenvalue crossings. A regularised version $\tilde{h}_\epsilon = \log(\epsilon + \rho(\widetilde{T}))$ or using the log-sum-exp as a smooth max over eigenvalues may be needed.

3. **How should $\beta$ (softening temperature) be set for $\sigma(\beta z_i)$?** Too small: soft regions blur across boundaries, destroying the piecewise-linear structure. Too large: recovers the hard indicator, losing differentiability. Anneal $\beta$ from small to large during training, similar to the $\alpha$ annealing in teacher forcing.

4. **Can the QR orthogonalisation in SLAO be extended to the full AL-RNN** (not just $W_{:,:P}$)? The linear core $W_{P:,P:}$ and the diagonal $A$ also need treatment for tasks where the Koopman spectrum shifts. Apply SLAO independently to both the nonlinear columns ($B = W_{:,:P}$) and the linear-core block ($W_{P:,P:}$)?

5. **Boundary behaviour test** (from `SC_RNN.md` §4.3): sweep a parameter toward a bifurcation and check the assimilate→accommodate decision flips at the topological-type change. The signature-Fisher should show a peak in $F_i^\Phi$ at the bifurcation (where $\partial\tilde{h}/\partial\theta_i$ is large), validating that the regularizer is most active exactly at schema boundaries.

## 8. Implementation: Precise Mathematical Description

The implementation lives in [`piagets.py`](piagets.py) and [`alrnn.py`](alrnn.py). All notation corresponds exactly to the code.

### 8.1 AL-RNN with SLAO factorization (from [`alrnn.py`](alrnn.py))

The model is an AL-RNN with $M$ latent units, $P$ nonlinear (ReLU) units, $d$ observed dimensions, and LoRA rank $r$ (default $r = P$):

$$z_{t+1} = A \odot z_t + g(z_t)\,W_A^\top W_B^\top + h, \qquad g(z)_i = \begin{cases} \mathrm{relu}(z_i) & i < P \\ z_i & i \geq P \end{cases}$$

**Parameters**: $A \in \mathbb{R}^M$ (diagonal recurrence), $W_B \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r}$ (up-projection), $W_A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times M}$ (down-projection), $h \in \mathbb{R}^M$ (bias). The effective full coupling matrix is $W = W_B W_A \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times M}$, computed on demand via `model.W()`.

The step is equivalent to $g(z_t)\,W^\top$ with $W = W_B W_A$, but is computed in the factored form $g(z_t) @ W_A^\top @ W_B^\top$ to keep gradients clean. States are clipped: $z_{t+1} \leftarrow \mathrm{clip}(z_{t+1}, -c, c)$, default $c=8$.

### 8.2 Differentiable signature $\phi(\theta)$ — `diff_phi`

`diff_phi` returns a vector $\phi(\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^{D}$ with $D = (M-P) + 2P + 2 = 24$ (for $M=16$, $P=6$), whose gradient flows through $(W_B, W_A, A, h)$.

**Warmup**: run the model for $T_\text{warm}$ steps under `torch.no_grad()` from $z=0$ to settle on the attractor. The final state is detached.

**Tracked rollout**: run $T_\text{track}$ further steps with gradient tracking through $(W_B, W_A, A, h)$:
$$z_{t+1} = A \odot z_t + g(z_t)\, W_A^\top W_B^\top + h, \qquad s_t = \sigma(\beta\, z_{t,0:P}) \in [0,1]^P$$

Default: $T_\text{warm} = 200$, $T_\text{track} = 300$, $\beta = 10$.

**Component (a) — linear core eigenvalue magnitudes** ($M-P = 10$ values):

$$W = W_B W_A \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times M}, \qquad B_\text{lin} = \mathrm{diag}(A_{P:}) + W_{P:,\,P:}$$
$$\phi^{(a)} = \bigl|\lambda\!\left(B_\text{lin}\right)\bigr| \in \mathbb{R}^{M-P}$$

The $(M-P)\times(M-P)$ linear-unit sub-block of $W$ is extracted from the product $W_B W_A$. Gradient flows through both factors.

**Component (b) — soft activation rates** ($P = 6$ values):

$$\phi^{(b)}_i = \frac{1}{T_\text{track}} \sum_{t=1}^{T_\text{track}} s_{t,i}$$

Time-averaged probability that ReLU unit $i$ is active. Encodes the occupation measure of the switching partition. For Lorenz (1-region collapse, all $P$ units in a fixed gate state): $\phi^{(b)}_i \approx 0.88$ (near-constant high activation). For Torus/VdP (multi-region oscillation): values are more varied.

**Component (c) — soft activation variability** ($P = 6$ values):

$$\phi^{(c)}_i = \mathrm{std}_t\, \sigma(\beta\, z_{i,t})$$

Standard deviation of the soft gate over the tracked trajectory. This is the differentiable counterpart of the discrete `n_regions` count:
- **1-region attractor** (Lorenz): each $\sigma(\beta z_i)$ stays nearly constant, so $\phi^{(c)}_i \approx 0$.
- **Multi-region oscillator** (Torus, VdP): units repeatedly cross $z_i=0$, so $\phi^{(c)}_i > 0$.

This adds 6 dimensions that strongly compress within-Lorenz spread in $\phi$-space (all Lorenz variants share $\phi^{(c)} \approx 0$) while widening inter-class gaps to Torus/VdP.

**Component (d) — soft topological entropy** (1 value):

$$\widetilde{T}_{ij} = \frac{1}{T_\text{track}-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_\text{track}-1} s_{t,i}\, s_{t+1,j} \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times P}$$
$$\phi^{(d)} = \log\!\bigl(\rho(\widetilde{T}) + 10^{-6}\bigr)$$

$\rho(\widetilde{T})$ is the spectral radius of the soft transition matrix, computed via `torch.linalg.eigvals`. Approximates topological entropy of the symbolic dynamics.

**Component (e) — max Lyapunov exponent** (1 value):

Computed via power iteration on the soft Jacobian. At each step the Jacobian is approximated as:
$$\widetilde{J}_t = \mathrm{diag}(A) + \mathrm{diag}\bigl(\sigma(\beta z_{t,<P})\oplus \mathbf{1}_{M-P}\bigr) \cdot W$$

A fixed unit perturbation $\delta z$ is propagated as $\delta z \leftarrow \widetilde{J}_t \delta z / \|\widetilde{J}_t \delta z\|$, accumulating $\phi^{(e)} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \log\|\widetilde{J}_t \delta z\|$. Gradient flows through $W$ and $A$ at every step. The initial $\delta z$ is fixed (deterministic, all-ones direction) for consistent $\phi$ values across calls.

Positive $\phi^{(e)}$: chaotic dynamics. Negative: contracting/linear-regime — Lorenz models in 1-region operation produce $\phi^{(e)} \approx -0.03$ to $-0.04$ (linear collapse), while Torus/VdP produce values near zero or positive.

**Full signature:**
$$\phi(\theta) = \bigl[\phi^{(a)};\; \phi^{(b)};\; \phi^{(c)};\; \phi^{(d)};\; \phi^{(e)}\bigr] \in \mathbb{R}^{D}, \qquad D = (M-P) + 2P + 2 = 24$$

### 8.3 Signature-Fisher — `signature_fisher`

$$\hat{F}_i^\Phi = \frac{1}{n_\text{avg}} \sum_{r=1}^{n_\text{avg}} \sum_{k=0}^{D-1} \left(\frac{\partial \phi_k^{(r)}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right)^{\!2}$$

$\phi^{(r)}$ is one call to `diff_phi`; the outer loop averages $n_\text{avg}$ independent rollouts. Each $\partial\phi_k/\partial\theta_i$ is computed via `torch.autograd.grad(phi[k], params)` — one backward per component, `retain_graph=True` for $k < D-1$. Code default $n_\text{avg} = 5$; experiments use $n_\text{avg} = 3$ (set via `SIG_FISHER_KW`). Cost: $3 \times 24 = 72$ backward passes per Fisher estimate.

### 8.4 Prediction-Fisher — `pred_fisher`

Standard diagonal EWC Fisher from the reconstruction MSE (baseline method):

$$\hat{F}_i^\text{pred} = \frac{1}{N_b} \sum_{b=1}^{N_b} \left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_b(\theta)}{\partial \theta_i}\right)^{\!2}$$

Random contiguous batches of length 100, GTF with $\alpha=0.05$. Default $N_b = 30$.

### 8.5 Fisher normalisation

$$\hat{F}_{\theta_i} \leftarrow \frac{\max(F_{\theta_i},\; 0)}{\max_j F_{\theta_j} + 10^{-12}} \in [0, 1]$$

Applied independently per parameter tensor. Ensures EWC penalty scale is comparable across tasks and parameter types.

### 8.6 SLAO QR initialisation — `PIAGETSContinual.qr_init`

SLAO lines 3–4 applied to the full $W = W_B W_A$ factorization.

At the start of task $t > 0$, the previous task's fine-tuned $W_A^{(t-1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times M}$ (wide matrix) is used to initialize $W_A^{(t)}$ with orthonormal rows:

$$Q_{t-1}, R_{t-1} = \mathrm{QR}\!\left(\left(W_A^{(t-1)}\right)^\top\right), \qquad Q_{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r},\; R_{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$$

Absorb the sign ambiguity of QR into the columns of $Q$:

$$Q_{t-1} \;\leftarrow\; Q_{t-1} \odot \mathrm{sign}\!\left(\mathrm{diag}(R_{t-1})\right)^\top$$

Then set:

$$W_A^{(0)}_t = Q_{t-1}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times M} \quad \text{(orthonormal rows — fresh projection directions)}$$
$$W_B^{(0)}_t = W_B^{(t-1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r} \quad \text{(carry previous up-projection unchanged)}$$

**Intuition**: $W_A$ projects the full $M$-dim activation $g(z)$ down to rank-$r$ space. After task $t-1$, its row-space is some $r$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^M$. QR on $W_A^\top$ finds an orthonormal basis for that subspace; $Q^\top$ re-initialises $W_A$ to have those orthonormal rows, giving a canonical re-entry point that is close to the previous task's subspace but with zero redundancy between rows. $W_B$ is held fixed because it is the parameter accumulated across tasks by EMA (SLAO line 7).

### 8.7 EWC loss on $W_B$ only — `PIAGETSContinual.ewc_loss`

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{EWC}(\theta) = \lambda_\text{EWC} \sum_{i,j} \hat{F}_{ij}^{W_B} \cdot \bigl(W_{B,ij} - \hat{W}_{B,ij}\bigr)^2$$

where $\hat{W}_B$ is the EMA-consolidated anchor and $\hat{F}^{W_B}$ is the EMA-consolidated Fisher for $W_B$ (both $\in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r}$). Only $W_B$ is protected — $W_A$, $A$, and $h$ are unconstrained. This is deliberate: $W_A$ is re-initialised by `qr_init` at every task boundary, so penalising it is counterproductive; $W_B$ carries the cumulative task history and is what SLAO's EMA merge is protecting.

**Fisher and anchor consolidation** (EMA, done inside `store_task`):

$$\hat{F}^{W_B} \;\leftarrow\; \hat{F}^{W_B} + \lambda_t \bigl(F^{(t)}_{W_B} - \hat{F}^{W_B}\bigr)$$
$$\hat{W}_B \;\leftarrow\; \hat{W}_B + \lambda_t \bigl(W_B^{(t)} - \hat{W}_B\bigr), \qquad \lambda_t = 1/\sqrt{t}$$

This prevents $O(T)$ growth of the EWC sum across tasks (a known failure mode of vanilla accumulated EWC).

### 8.8 Task storage and SLAO merge — `PIAGETSContinual.store_task`

Called after fine-tuning completes on task $t$ with the just-trained parameters $(W_A^{(t)}, W_B^{(t)})$:

1. **Compute Fisher** on $W_B$ via `signature_fisher` or `pred_fisher` → $F^{(t)}_{W_B} \in [0,1]^{M \times r}$.

2. **Consolidate EMA Fisher and anchor** (see §8.7).

3. **SLAO line 6 — $A_\text{merge}$ direct replace**:
   $$A_\text{merge} = W_A^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times M}$$

4. **SLAO line 7 — Fisher-weighted $B_\text{merge}$ update**:
   $$\alpha_{ij} = \lambda_t \cdot \bigl(1 - \hat{F}^{W_B}_{ij}\bigr), \qquad \lambda_t = 1/\sqrt{t}$$
   $$B_\text{merge} \;\leftarrow\; B_\text{merge} + \alpha \odot \bigl(W_B^{(t)} - B_\text{merge}\bigr)$$

   High Fisher importance ($\hat{F}_{ij} \to 1$) → $\alpha_{ij} \to 0$ (element protected). Low Fisher ($\hat{F}_{ij} \to 0$) → $\alpha_{ij} = \lambda_t$ (standard SLAO EMA rate). This is the per-element extension of SLAO's scalar EMA, driven by the consolidated signature-Fisher. Note: this differs slightly from the theoretical proposal in §2.3 (which uses $\lambda_t/(1+\beta F)$); the implementation uses the linear form $\lambda_t(1-F)$ which is simpler and equivalent for $F \in [0,1]$.

5. **Save** $W_B^{(t)} \to $ `_B_prev` and $W_A^{(t)} \to$ `_A_prev` for next task's `qr_init`.

6. **Compute** $\phi(\theta_t) = \texttt{diff\_phi}(\theta_t)$.  Then, if `probe_for_next=True`, run `schema_probe` against the **currently stored** `_task_phis` (not yet including task $t$). Cache the result as `_next_lam_ewc` for the next task. **Critically: the probe runs before step 7**, so the current task's $\phi$ is not yet in the library — otherwise the probe would always find distance 0 to itself and always return ASSIMILATION.

7. **Append** $(c_t, \phi(\theta_t))$ to `_task_phis` for future schema comparisons.

**Inference** (`restore_merged`): set $W_B \leftarrow B_\text{merge}$, $W_A \leftarrow A_\text{merge}$ so the model uses the accumulated cross-task LoRA.

### 8.9 Training loop — `train_with_ewc`

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{total} = \mathcal{L}_\text{recon}(\theta;\, \alpha_e) + \mathcal{L}_\text{EWC}(\theta) + \mathcal{L}_\phi(\theta)$$

$\mathcal{L}_\text{recon}$ is GTF-BPTT MSE plus the region-entropy regularizer $\lambda_\text{reg} \cdot H_\text{reg}$. The GTF coefficient is linearly annealed: $\alpha_e = \alpha_0 + (\alpha_\text{end}-\alpha_0) e/(E-1)$ from $\alpha_0=0.5$ to $\alpha_\text{end}=0.05$. $\mathcal{L}_\phi = 0$ by default; activated for the `piagets_phi_reg` and `piagets_combined` variants (§8.12). Optional dual-LoRA gradient masking (§8.13) is applied after `loss.backward()` and before `opt.step()` for the `piagets_dual_lora` and `piagets_combined` variants.

### 8.10 Schema probe and adaptive $\lambda$ — `PIAGETSContinual.schema_probe`

The probe compares the current model's $\phi$ against known class centroids to decide whether the upcoming task belongs to an existing schema (assimilation) or is new (accommodation):

1. Compute $\phi_\text{now}$ as the mean of `n_avg` calls to `diff_phi`.

2. For each known class $c$, compute the centroid $\bar\phi_c = \mathrm{mean}_{(c',\phi') \in \text{task\_phis},\, c'=c} \phi'$.

3. Compute the mean **intra-class spread** $\sigma_\text{intra} = \mathrm{mean}_i \|\phi_i - \bar\phi_{c_i}\| / \sigma_\phi$ (mean φ-distance from each stored task to its own class centroid).

4. Find nearest class: $c^* = \arg\min_c \|\phi_\text{now} - \bar\phi_c\| / \sigma_\phi$, distance $d^*$.

5. **Assimilation** if $d^* < \sigma_\text{schema} \cdot \max(\sigma_\text{intra}, 0.1)$; else **accommodation**.

6. Return $(\lambda_\text{assim}, \text{"assimilation"}, c^*)$ or $(\lambda_\text{accom}, \text{"accommodation"}, \text{None})$.

**Probe-for-next timing** (the correct approach): The probe runs inside `store_task`, **before** appending the current task's $\phi$ to `_task_phis`. This ordering is critical: if the current task's $\phi$ were appended first, the probe would find it with distance 0 and always return ASSIMILATION. By probing before append, we ask "does this just-trained model's $\phi$ resemble any **previously known** schema?" The result is cached as `_next_lam_ewc`. At the start of the next task, `qr_init` applies this cached $\lambda$ rather than probing the pre-initialisation (wrong-task) model. This ensures the probe measures the schema of the task we just learned — which is the relevant information for deciding how protective to be while learning the next task.

**Adaptive rule:**
- Assimilation detected → $\lambda_\text{EWC} \leftarrow \lambda_\text{assim} = 20$ (high protection: don't destroy a confirmed existing schema)
- Accommodation detected → $\lambda_\text{EWC} \leftarrow \lambda_\text{accom} = 4$ (low protection: allow plasticity for genuinely new structure)

**Current limitation**: the probe is based on the just-trained model's φ, which reflects what was just learned, not what will be learned next. If tasks t and t+1 are different classes, the probe correctly identifies class $c_t$ and we get the right λ for protecting $c_t$ during $t+1$ training. But the probe can't know whether $t+1$ will be in the same class — it only tells us how to protect the past. The correct framing is: *"we've confirmed we're in class X — apply high λ to protect X from the upcoming task"*, which is exactly what probe-for-next does.

### 8.11 Evaluation metrics — `validate_piagets.py`

**Performance matrix**: after training task $t$,
$$R_{t,i} = -\,\phi\text{-dist}\!\left(\phi(\theta_t),\; \phi_i^\text{ora}\right), \quad i \leq t, \qquad \phi\text{-dist}(a, b) = \left\|\frac{a - b}{\sigma_\phi}\right\|_2$$
Higher $R_{t,i}$ = model at step $t$ is closer to oracle $i$'s attractor in $\phi$-space.

**BWT** (backward transfer): $\mathrm{BWT} = \frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{i=0}^{T-2}(R_{T-1,i} - R_{i,i})$. Negative = forgetting; zero = perfect retention.

**FWT** (forward transfer): $\mathrm{FWT} = \frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{T-1}(R_{i,i} - R^\text{rand}_i)$ vs an untrained random-init baseline. Negative here is expected (the model initialises cold for each method; no warm-start benefit).

**rec\_ora** (nearest-task-oracle recognition): at each step $t$, find $i^* = \arg\min_{i \leq t}\phi\text{-dist}(\phi(\theta_t), \phi^\text{ora}_i)$; predict class $= \text{CLASSES}[i^*]$; score 1 if correct. Averaged over $T$ steps. This is the unbiased metric.

**rec\_cen** (nearest-class-centroid recognition): predict class $= \arg\min_c \phi\text{-dist}(\phi(\theta_t), \bar\phi_c^\text{ora})$ where $\bar\phi_c^\text{ora}$ is the mean oracle $\phi$ for class $c$. Biased when intra-class $\phi$-variance is high (heterogeneous parameterisations produce centroids that don't represent any individual task well).

### 8.12 φ-Functional Regularization variant — `piagets_phi_reg`

Option 3: supplement EWC with a direct penalty on attractor-structure change in φ-space.

**Per-class φ\* storage**: After each task $t$ of class $c$, `store_task` maintains a 24-dim per-class anchor via EMA:
$$\phi^*(c) \;\leftarrow\; \tfrac{1}{2}\phi^*(c) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\phi(\theta_t)$$
initialised from the first task of class $c$. Storage cost: 3 × 24 = 72 floats total.

**Penalty** (added to training loss alongside $\mathcal{L}_\text{EWC}$):
$$\mathcal{L}_\phi = \lambda_\phi \,\left\|\phi(\theta) - \phi^*(c_t)\right\|^2$$
Silent on the first occurrence of each class (no $\phi^*$ yet). Default $\lambda_\phi = 0.05$; `diff_phi` called with shorter rollout ($T_\text{warm}=100$, $T_\text{track}=100$) for speed.

**Properties**:
- *Gauge-invariant*: $\phi(\theta)$ is computed from trajectory statistics, so $\mathcal{L}_\phi$ does not penalise latent-basis rotations that leave the attractor shape unchanged. Standard EWC on $W_B$ is not gauge-invariant.
- *Semantically targeted*: penalises shifts in eigenvalue spectrum, activation-switching patterns, topological entropy, and Lyapunov exponent — precisely the quantities PIAGETS uses for schema discrimination.
- *All-parameter gradient*: $\partial\phi/\partial\theta$ covers $W_B$, $W_A$, $A$, and $h$ jointly, whereas $\mathcal{L}_\text{EWC}$ protects only $W_B$.

### 8.13 Dual LoRA Block Split variant — `piagets_dual_lora`

Option 4: partition $W_B$ columns into a linear-core block (Lorenz tasks) and a nonlinear-coupling block (Torus/VdP tasks), updating only the active block per task. The frozen block suffers zero gradient-based forgetting by construction.

**Block structure** (with $r=6$, $r_\text{lin}=3$):

| Block | $W_B$ columns | $W_A$ rows | Active for |
|---|---|---|---|
| linear-core | $0:r_\text{lin}$ (cols 0–2) | $0:r_\text{lin}$ (rows 0–2) | Lorenz (class 0) |
| nonlinear-coupling | $r_\text{lin}:r$ (cols 3–5) | $r_\text{lin}:r$ (rows 3–5) | Torus, VdP (classes 1, 2) |

**Regime routing**: assigned by class label in `validate_piagets.py` (class 0 → "linear"; classes 1/2 → "nonlinear"). The label-free alternative uses `detect_regime(model)`: compute $\phi^{(c)}.mean()$ (soft activation variability); if $< 0.05$ → "linear", else → "nonlinear".

**Gradient masking** (in `apply_block_mask`, called after `loss.backward()` before `opt.step()`):
- Linear regime: $\nabla_{W_B}[\;:,\; r_\text{lin}:] \leftarrow 0$, $\nabla_{W_A}[r_\text{lin}:\;,\; :] \leftarrow 0$
- Nonlinear regime: $\nabla_{W_B}[\;:,\; :r_\text{lin}] \leftarrow 0$, $\nabla_{W_A}[:r_\text{lin},\; :] \leftarrow 0$

**Limitation** (see §9.7): at $r_\text{lin}=3$ each regime has only half the LoRA rank, which is insufficient for chaotic attractors. The method underperforms at the current $r=6$; a larger rank (e.g.\ $r=8$, $r_\text{lin}=4$) would give each block adequate capacity.

## 9. Experimental Results

### 9.0 Metric definitions

All metrics derive from the performance matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times T}$ (lower-triangular; $R_{t,i}$ defined only for $i \leq t$):
$$R_{t,i} = -\phi\text{-dist}\!\left(\phi(\theta_t),\; \phi_i^\text{ora}\right), \qquad \phi\text{-dist}(a,b) = \left\|\frac{a-b}{\sigma_\phi}\right\|_2$$
Higher (less negative) $R_{t,i}$ means the CL model at step $t$ is closer to oracle $i$'s attractor in normalised φ-space.

| Metric | Formula | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **BWT** | $\frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{i=0}^{T-2}(R_{T-1,i}-R_{i,i})$ | *Backward transfer*: did the model's fit to past tasks improve (positive) or degrade (negative) between training time and the end of the stream? Negative = forgetting; zero = perfect retention. |
| **FWT** | $\frac{1}{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{T-1}(R_{i,i}-R_i^\text{rand})$ | *Forward transfer*: does prior task training help on the current task compared to a random-init model? |
| **rec\_ora** | $\frac{1}{T}\sum_t\mathbb{1}[\text{CLASSES}[i^*]=c_t],\; i^*=\arg\min_{i\leq t}\phi\text{-dist}(\phi_t,\phi_i^\text{ora})$ | *Nearest-task-oracle recognition*: at each step, predict class by finding the closest individual oracle in φ-space. Unbiased — avoids centroid-averaging artefacts. |
| **rec\_cen** | $\frac{1}{T}\sum_t\mathbb{1}[\hat c=c_t],\;\hat c=\arg\min_c\phi\text{-dist}(\phi_t,\bar\phi_c^\text{ora})$ | *Nearest-class-centroid recognition*: predict class by distance to the mean oracle φ per class. Biased when within-class φ-variance is high. |
| **R\_diag mean** | $\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}R_{t,t}$ | *Mean training-time performance*: how well the model fits each task immediately after training on it. Unaffected by forgetting dynamics; lower (more negative) values indicate over-regularisation or insufficient training. |

**BWT metric bias**: BWT rewards methods that under-fit at training time. A method with high EWC λ fits task $i$ poorly (low $R_{i,i}$, large magnitude), leaving more room for apparent "backward improvement" at final evaluation even when the final absolute performance $R[T-1,i]$ is comparable. Methods with adaptive λ that fit tasks well initially (high $R_{i,i}$, small magnitude) show smaller positive BWT credit for the same final quality. R\_diag mean and final absolute values $R[T-1,i]$ are more reliable quality indicators.

### 9.5 Why pred\_ewc's BWT appears higher despite comparable recognition

BWT = mean$_{i<T}(R[T-1,i] - R[i,i])$. The formula rewards methods that fit poorly at training time (low R[i,i]) but recover later. pred\_ewc uses a fixed λ=10 for all tasks; piagets-family methods use adaptive λ ∈ {4,20}. When accommodation tasks (new class) get λ=4, the model fits well (high R[i,i]) but leaves less room for BWT credit. Concretely for Lor\_r35 (t=3):

| | R[3,3] | R[9,3] | BWT contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| pred\_ewc | −11.390 | −7.665 | +3.725 |
| piagets | −8.245 | −7.718 | +0.527 |

pred\_ewc starts worse (−11.390 vs −8.245) and "improves" by 3.7 units. piagets fits the task well initially (−8.245) and retains it almost as well (−7.718 vs −7.665 for pred\_ewc) — the final absolute performance is nearly identical, but BWT credit is much smaller.

This bias means BWT as defined is not a reliable metric for comparing methods with different λ schedules. The rec\_ora metric (schema class recognition) and final absolute performance (R[T-1,i]) are better indicators.

## 10. Status and Remaining Improvements

### 10.1 What has been implemented

**φ^{(c)} — soft activation variability (phi\_act\_std)** [implemented]: Added as component (c) in `diff_phi`, making φ 24-dimensional. Effect: within-Lorenz spread compresses (all Lorenz share φ^{(c)}≈0), Lorenz–Torus and Lorenz–VdP oracle gaps widen to 7.8–10.5. Torus–VdP minimum gap increased to 4.045.

**Probe-before-append ordering** [implemented]: The schema probe in `store_task` now runs BEFORE appending the current task's φ to `_task_phis`, preventing the probe from always finding distance 0 to itself.

**lam\_accom fallback for empty-tasks list** [implemented]: `schema_probe` returns `lam_accom` when no prior tasks are stored.

**σ\_schema = 2.0** [implemented]: Widened assimilation window; marginally improves piagets\_adaptive.

**φ-functional regularization** [implemented, §8.12]: `phi_reg_loss(model, current_class)` computes L\_φ = λ\_phi ‖φ(θ)−φ\*(class)‖². Per-class φ\* stored via EMA in `store_task`. Finding: additive φ-reg (alongside EWC) hurts BWT vs base piagets at current λ settings because it conflicts with SLAO B\_merge accumulation.

**Dual LoRA block split** [implemented, §8.13]: `apply_block_mask` zeros gradients for frozen block after backward. Asymmetric R\_LIN=2 (rank-2 for Lorenz, rank-4 for Torus/VdP) improves over symmetric R\_LIN=3. Still shows negative BWT at r=6.

**Per-class EWC anchors** [implemented, §8.12 option 5]: `use_class_ewc=True` maintains separate Fisher and W\_B\* per class. Finding: gives best training-time performance (FWT +2.96, diagonal −8.94) but negative BWT (−2.02) because cross-class W\_B is unprotected.

**φ-only mode** [implemented]: `lam_ewc=0, lam_phi=0.15` — φ-reg as the sole CL mechanism. Good plasticity but insufficient retention on its own.

**Adaptive λ calibration** [key finding]: The most impactful discovery across all runs. Reducing LAM\_EWC from 10→5 and LAM\_ACCOM from 4→2 transformed piagets\_adaptive from BWT +0.60/rec\_ora 0.50 to BWT +2.24/rec\_ora 0.90. The schema probe becomes highly effective only when the accommodation branch is genuinely plastic (λ\_accom much less than λ\_EWC), making the assimilation/accommodation distinction meaningful in practice.

### 10.2 Core remaining problem: single-example class recognition

The probe threshold is $\tau = \sigma \cdot \max(\text{intra\_mean}, 0.1)$. When only 1 instance of a class has been stored, intra\_mean for that class is 0, and the mean intra\_mean is diluted by those zeros. With 3 classes and 1 task each, intra\_mean = 0 clamped to 0.1, and $\tau = \sigma \times 0.1 \approx 0.2$. This threshold is far too small: no CL model is within 0.2 φ-units of its stored oracle-trained twin (they differ by EWC training artifacts).

The threshold only becomes meaningful after ≥2 examples of the same class are stored (non-zero intra-class spread). By then, many critical λ decisions have already been made wrongly (as ACCOMMODATION when ASSIMILATION was needed), and the damage to W\_B accumulates.

**Fix A — minimum per-class threshold**: Replace `max(intra_mean, 0.1)` with `max(intra_mean, min_class_sigma)` where `min_class_sigma` is calibrated from oracle φ-distances. From the oracle matrix, within-class minimum distances are: Lorenz 3.056 (Lor\_r35↔Lor\_r40), Torus 2.707 (Tor\_r382↔Tor\_r618), VdP 3.955 (VdP\_m1.5↔VdP\_m5.0). A global `min_class_sigma ≈ 1.5` in normalised units would give threshold ≈ 3.0 even for single-example classes — sufficient to catch same-class models within their oracle distance.

**Fix B — prior-injected threshold**: On the first encounter of a new class (intra\_mean=0), use a separate calibration σ\_prior computed from oracle distances (or set by the user as domain knowledge). Only fall back to data-driven intra\_mean once ≥2 examples exist.

### 10.3 Per-class B\_merge (class-conditional EMA)

Currently a single global B\_merge accumulates all tasks. Because Lorenz arrives 4× and produces high-Fisher B weights, it crowds out Torus and VdP patterns. The restore\_merged evaluation then shows poor retention for minority classes.

**Fix**: maintain one B\_merge per schema class, keyed by the class label from the probe. Before training task t, restore B\_merge[c\*] where c\* is the probe result. After `store_task`, update only B\_merge[c\_t]. For evaluation, restore B\_merge[c\_i] for each task i. This eliminates cross-class contamination without changing the EWC or QR structure.

### 10.4 Waveform spectral component in φ

Torus and VdP share near-zero topological entropy, keeping Torus–VdP inter-class gaps at 4.0–6.5. Adding spectral bandpower of the free-run trajectory sharpens the distinction:

$$\phi^\text{spec}_i = \frac{\int_{f_\text{low}}^{f_\text{high}} |Z_i(f)|^2 df}{\text{Var}(z_i)}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, P$$

VdP limit cycles are dominated by a single fundamental frequency with strong overtones (relaxation waveform). Torus trajectories carry two incommensurate frequencies. The ratio of power in the first harmonic to total power distinguishes them reliably. The spectral component is computed from the free-run trajectory, fully autodifferentiable through `torch.fft.rfft`, and gauge-invariant (rotation of observation axes changes channel identity but not the frequency content of individual latent units).

### 10.5 Margin-weighted adaptive λ

The current binary adaptive λ (assim=20, accom=4) does not account for how confident the probe is. A continuous version:

$$\lambda_t = \lambda_\text{base} \cdot \exp\!\left(\alpha \cdot \frac{\tau - d^*}{\tau}\right)$$

where $d^*$ is the distance to the nearest class centroid and $\tau$ is the threshold. Near the boundary ($d^* \approx \tau$): λ≈λ\_base (moderate). Deep inside a known class ($d^* \ll \tau$): λ grows (strong protection). Far outside all known classes ($d^* \gg \tau$): ACCOMMODATION with λ=λ\_accom. This provides a smooth version of the binary switch, with naturally wider protection for high-confidence assimilation decisions.
